Ancient Artifact Gripper

One of our projects for our Design class. We were tasked with recovering an "ancient human artifact", an object that our professor picks every year. This year our artifact was the world's "first fidget spinner". To be exact, it was actually a large aluminum fidget spinner dummy. Our mission was to build a gripper that would be strong enough to hold this artifact. Not only did it need to have the strength to grip the object and lift it but, it also hold the objects tightly as it swung like a pendulum 3 times.




Our team's gripper had to fit under a motor. With certain geometric constraints vertically and horizontally. Additionally, we couldn't grip the object from the bottom, since it was considered a "restricted" zone. So we built a design that avoided these physical constraints and completed the task successfully.



My group and I knew that our biggest challenge was going to translate the motor's torque to a linear force of the contacts that would press against the sides of the fidget spinner and hold it while it swings. Thus we came up with a design that optimizes this conversion, which uses large bevel gears in order to be able to multiply our torque as well as translate our our motion to grip the fidget spinner.


Acrylic Mechanical Robot: G.O.A.T

Another project for our Design I project. This time we were tasked with creating a mechanical robot with a "starter kit" created by the course teaching staff. We are also provided with 3 different test stages. Rocks, stairs, and hurdles. We used all that data to design our robot to clear all the stages together.

Our procedure includes analyzing an animal in nature and then, modeling our robot after it. Our team chose the goat, due to its ability to climb rough terrain and stablity. We found a goat video online, and traced coupler curves of its movement. We then used MotionGen to model those coupler curves and ended up with a Roshaf-Crank Rocker, that simulated a goat walking. We built our robot and optomized other elements like, the legs to make what we believe to be the best design. Below is a full presentation of our process with all necessary data.

Urban Search and Rescue

A project in 16-311, our Introduction to Robotics class. We were tasked with and open ended project to produce a robot that would be run in a challenge Urban Search and Rescue simulation. Our robot had to have a robust navigation ability, with a design optimal to what we considered the most imporant things that an urban search and rescue robot need. Additionally, the robot had to have a microcontroller subsystem that see and project lights that used to open doors as well as a light for its own navigation. Cameras were provided as the main way to percieve the robot's location and control it remotely. We had a checkpoint that then culminated in a final competition where we ended up in the finalist round. We were allowed to use anything from our provided lego mindstorms kit as well as anything 3D printed or machined as long as long as it met our gemotric constraints as well as passed a drop test.

Our robot was optimized to climb the stairs in our challenge course. Our team thought that if we could clear the stairs, we would be able to clear most "rubble" and navigation obstacles that were presented to us. We also knew that lighting would be key to give us the best camera feed that in turn provided us with the best possible navigations, so we focused on those two things and ended up doing really well in the competion and in the assignment.
